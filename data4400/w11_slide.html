Part 1: Bayesian Fundamentals

Prior vs Posterior Knowledge

Prior Knowledge

  • What we believe before seeing data
  • Based on experience, intuition, historical patterns
  • Example: "I think 30% of customers will buy premium products"

Posterior Knowledge

  • Updated belief after observing data
  • Combines prior belief with evidence
  • Example: "After analyzing sales data, I now believe 35% will buy premium"
Business Translation: Start with your best guess, then let data improve your understanding.

Bayes' Theorem: The Foundation

P(H|E) = P(E|H)×P(H) P(E)

In Business Terms:

  • P(H|E): Updated belief after seeing evidence (Posterior)
  • P(E|H): How likely is this evidence if our belief is true? (Likelihood)
  • P(H): Our initial belief (Prior)
  • P(E): How likely is this evidence overall? (Normalizing constant)
Remember: This formula tells us how to update our beliefs when we get new information.

Quiz 1: Retail Boutique Scenario

Situation: A boutique owner believes that 30% of weekend customers will purchase premium items (prior belief).

New Evidence: A customer has been browsing for more than 10 minutes.

Customer Type% of TotalProbability of 10+ min Browsing
Premium Buyers30%80%
Regular Buyers70%20%

Question: What's the updated probability that this customer will buy premium items?

Think about it: How does the browsing behavior change your confidence about this customer's purchasing intent?

Breaking Down the Components

1. Prior P(H) = 0.30

Initial belief: 30% of customers buy premium items

2. Likelihood P(E|H)

  • P(10+ min browsing | Premium buyer) = 0.80
  • P(10+ min browsing | Regular buyer) = 0.20

3. Evidence P(E)

P(10+ min browsing) = (0.30 × 0.80) + (0.70 × 0.20) = 0.38

4. Posterior P(H|E)

P(Premium|10min) = 0.80×0.30 0.38 =0.63
Result: Confidence increased from 30% to 63% based on browsing behavior!

Business Translation of Bayesian Components

Prior = Initial Business Belief

Based on experience, market research, historical data, or industry benchmarks

Data = Market Evidence

New observations, customer behavior, sales patterns, or external indicators

Posterior = Updated Decision Confidence

Refined belief that combines experience with current evidence

Key Insight: Bayesian thinking provides a systematic way to update business assumptions as new data becomes available.

Bayesian vs Frequentist Thinking

Frequentist Approach

  • Probability = long-run frequency
  • "If we repeat this 1000 times..."
  • Fixed parameters to be estimated
  • Requires large sample sizes

Bayesian Approach

  • Probability = degree of belief
  • "Given what we know now..."
  • Parameters have distributions
  • Works with small samples

Why Bayesian Suits Business

  • Uncertainty: Business decisions are made under uncertainty
  • Prior Knowledge: Managers have valuable experience to incorporate
  • Limited Data: Often can't wait for large sample sizes
  • Continuous Learning: Beliefs should update as markets change

Sequential Learning: How Beliefs Evolve

Key Principle: Today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior

The Learning Cycle

  1. Start with Prior: Initial business belief (Week 1)
  2. Collect Evidence: Observe market data (Week 1)
  3. Update to Posterior: Revised belief (End of Week 1)
  4. New Prior: Use revised belief as starting point (Week 2)
  5. Repeat Process: Continuous improvement

Business Example: E-commerce Conversion Rates

  • Month 1: Prior belief: 3% conversion rate
  • Month 1 Data: Observe actual conversions → Posterior: 3.2%
  • Month 2: Use 3.2% as new prior → Continue learning

Uncertainty Quantification

Confidence Intervals vs Credible Intervals

Confidence Intervals (Frequentist)

Interpretation: "If we repeated this study 100 times, 95 of the intervals would contain the true parameter."

Problem: Hard to explain to business stakeholders

Credible Intervals (Bayesian)

Interpretation: "There's a 95% probability that the true parameter lies in this range."

Advantage: Intuitive for business decision-making

Business Communication Example

Instead of: "The 95% confidence interval for quarterly sales is $2.3M to $2.7M"

Say: "We're 95% confident that quarterly sales will be between $2.3M and $2.7M"

Impact: Stakeholders can directly interpret the probability statement

Parameter Estimation with Uncertainty

Traditional Approach: "The growth rate is 5.2%"

Bayesian Approach: "The growth rate is likely between 4.8% and 5.6%, with 5.2% being most probable"

Why This Matters for Forecasting

  • Risk Assessment: Understand the range of possible outcomes
  • Decision Making: Consider worst-case and best-case scenarios
  • Resource Planning: Build in appropriate buffers
  • Communication: Set realistic stakeholder expectations

Assessment 3 Application

When presenting your forecasting model, include statements like:

  • "Based on our analysis, there's a 90% probability that demand will be between X and Y units"
  • "The most likely scenario is Z, but we should prepare for variations of ±15%"

Bayesian Model Selection

Question: Which forecasting model should I choose for my Assessment 3 project?

Bayesian Criteria for Model Comparison

  • Prior Predictive Checks: Does the model make sense before seeing data?
  • Posterior Predictive Checks: Can the model reproduce observed patterns?
  • Information Criteria: Balance fit quality with model complexity
  • Cross-Validation: How well does the model predict unseen data?

Practical Model Selection Process

  1. Define candidate models (e.g., linear trend vs. seasonal model)
  2. Set priors for each model based on business understanding
  3. Fit models to data and calculate model probabilities
  4. Choose model with highest posterior probability
  5. Justify choice in business terms for stakeholders

Sensitivity Analysis: Testing Prior Assumptions

Why Sensitivity Analysis Matters

  • Stakeholder Confidence: Show that conclusions are robust
  • Risk Management: Identify when priors strongly influence results
  • Transparency: Acknowledge uncertainty in initial assumptions
  • Decision Making: Understand when to collect more data

How to Perform Sensitivity Analysis

  1. Vary Prior Parameters: Test optimistic, realistic, and pessimistic scenarios
  2. Compare Posteriors: See how much conclusions change
  3. Identify Influential Priors: Which assumptions matter most?
  4. Report Robustness: Communicate stability of results
For Assessment 3: Include a brief sensitivity analysis to show your forecasts are reliable across reasonable assumption ranges.

Business Risk Assessment with Bayesian Forecasting

Traditional Risk Assessment

  • Single point forecast
  • "Sales will be $2.5M"
  • Binary thinking: right or wrong
  • Hard to plan for uncertainty

Bayesian Risk Assessment

  • Probability distributions
  • "Sales: 70% chance between $2.3M-$2.7M"
  • Quantified uncertainty
  • Scenario-based planning

Risk Management Applications

  • Inventory Planning: "Order enough for 80% confidence level"
  • Financial Planning: "Budget for expected value plus 20% buffer"
  • Capacity Planning: "Staff for 90th percentile demand scenario"
  • Investment Decisions: "ROI has 75% probability of exceeding 15%"

Setting Realistic Business Priors for A3

Sources for Business Priors

  • Historical Data: Past performance of similar organizations
  • Industry Benchmarks: Sector averages and standards
  • Expert Opinion: Manager experience and intuition
  • Literature Review: Academic studies and case studies
  • Market Research: Survey data and consumer insights

A3 Prior Setting Strategy

  1. Research Your Industry: Find typical performance ranges
  2. Interview Stakeholders: Get management estimates
  3. Use Comparable Businesses: Look for similar organizations
  4. Be Conservative: Use wider ranges when uncertain
  5. Document Sources: Justify your prior choices clearly
Remember: Weak priors (high uncertainty) let data dominate. Strong priors (low uncertainty) require strong justification.

Combining Historical Data with Business Knowledge

  • Hard to justify to stakeholders

Bayesian Integration

  • Combines data with expertise

Integration Strategy for A3

  • Start with Business Understanding: What does management expect?
  • Incorporate Historical Patterns: What do past data suggest?

Building Credibility in A3 Presentations

Goal: Make your forecasting approach convincing to business stakeholders

Credibility-Building Strategies

  • Transparent Methodology: Explain your reasoning process clearly
  • Acknowledge Uncertainty: Show you understand limitations
  • Provide Ranges: Give confidence intervals, not just point estimates
  • Validate Assumptions: Test key assumptions with sensitivity analysis
  • Use Business Language: Translate statistical concepts to business impact

Powerful Phrases for A3 Presentations

  • "Based on our analysis, there's an 80% probability that..."
  • "We tested this assumption and found that even if we're wrong by 20%..."
  • "The evidence suggests that our initial estimate should be updated from X to Y"
  • "While we can't eliminate uncertainty, we can quantify it and plan accordingly"

Optimal Actions with Probabilistic Forecasts

Traditional Decision Making

Process: Get single forecast → Make decision → Hope for the best

Problem: No consideration of uncertainty or alternative scenarios

Bayesian Decision Making

Process: Get probability distribution → Evaluate expected outcomes → Choose optimal action

Decision Framework

  1. Define Possible Actions: What choices does the organization have?
  2. Estimate Outcomes: What happens under each scenario?
  3. Calculate Expected Values: Weight outcomes by probabilities
  4. Consider Risk Tolerance: How much uncertainty can the organization handle?
  5. Choose Optimal Action: Maximize expected utility
A3 Application: Don't just forecast - recommend specific actions based on your probabilistic predictions.

Presenting Bayesian Results to Business Stakeholders

❌ Don't Say

  • "The posterior distribution shows..."
  • "Based on Bayesian inference..."
  • "The credible interval is..."
  • "We updated our priors..."

✓ Do Say

  • "Our analysis shows that..."
  • "Based on the evidence..."
  • "We're 90% confident that..."
  • "The data suggests we should revise our expectations..."

Effective Communication Structure

  1. Start with Business Question: What decision needs to be made?
  2. Explain Your Approach: How you combined experience with data
  3. Present Results Clearly: Most likely outcome plus uncertainty range
  4. Translate to Actions: What should the organization do?
  5. Address Questions: Be prepared to explain your reasoning
