Key Time Series Definitions

Period

The length of one complete cycle in a repeating pattern

Weekly sales: Period = 7 days

Frequency

How often an event occurs within a period

Monthly data: 12 observations per year

Realization

A single observed value from a time series

Sales in January: 150 units

Mean & Standard Deviation

Average value and measure of variability

Mean: Central tendency
Std Dev: How spread out the data is

Stationarity

When statistical properties don't change over time

Constant mean and variance

5 Components of Time Series

Time series can consist of all, or some of, these components:

1. Long-term Trend
2. Stationarity
3. Seasonality
4. Cyclical Pattern
5. Irregularity (Random Variation)

Let's explore each component in detail...

Component 1: Stationarity

Stationary Time Series

Definition: A time series where statistical properties (mean, variance) remain constant over time.

Key Features: Fluctuates around a constant mean with consistent variance

Component 2: Long-term Trend

Trend Component

Definition: A relatively smooth pattern that persists for more than one year.

Examples: Population growth, house prices, technology adoption

Direction: Can be upward, downward, or stable

Component 3: Seasonality

Seasonal Pattern

Definition: Repetitive behavior with a clearly defined and constant period.

Examples: Retail sales (holiday seasons), ice cream sales (summer peaks), heating costs (winter peaks)

Key Feature: Predictable pattern that repeats at regular intervals

Component 4: Cyclical Pattern

Cyclical Variation

Definition: A wavelike pattern with no precise period, describing long-term behavior.

Examples: Business cycles (recession/expansion), economic indicators

Key Difference: Unlike seasonality, cyclical patterns have irregular periods

Component 5: Random Variation (Irregularity)

Random/Irregular Component

Definition: Non-regular and unpredictable changes in the time series.

Examples: Natural disasters, unexpected events, measurement errors

Impact: Tends to hide other, more predictable components

Note: Often called "noise" in the data
