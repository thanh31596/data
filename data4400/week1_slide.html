What is Forecasting?

LO1 · Definition
Definition

Forecasting is a systematic process of predicting future values based on past and current information.

The key word is systematic — forecasting is not guessing. It uses structured methods and data to produce a prediction that can be evaluated and improved.

Businesses use forecasting to answer questions like:

  • How many customers will visit next month?
  • How much stock should we order?
  • When will our water supply fall below a critical level?
  • What will sales be in Q3?
The Three Time Zones of Forecasting
Past
Historical data
What actually happened — the evidence we learn from
Present
Current observations
What is happening now — the trigger for a decision
Future
The forecast
Our best estimate of what comes next, with uncertainty quantified

The Three Laws of Forecasting

LO1 · Fundamental Principles

Before learning any technique, accept these three facts. They apply to every forecast, from weather to water supply.

I

Forecasts are always wrong

No model can predict the exact future value. The goal is to be usefully close, not perfectly correct.

Example: Disney predicted summer 2025 crowds using historical data — but actual attendance still differed from the forecast.
II

Every forecast must include an error estimate

A forecast without uncertainty bounds is incomplete. Decision-makers need to know how much to trust a prediction.

Example: "We forecast 50,000 litres, ± 8,000" is far more useful than "We forecast 50,000 litres."
III

Short-term forecasts are more accurate than long-term ones

Accuracy degrades the further out you forecast. A one-month forecast is almost always better than a five-year forecast.

Example: Predicting tomorrow's dam level is easier than predicting next year's — too many unknowns accumulate over time.

Why Businesses Need Forecasting

LO1 · Business Value

Forecasting is not an academic exercise. It has direct financial consequences for any organisation.

Business Need Without Forecasting With Forecasting
Inventory management Over-order (waste money) or under-order (lose sales) Order the right amount at the right time
Staff scheduling Too many staff on quiet days; not enough on busy days Match staffing to predicted demand
Capital investment Invest in capacity based on gut feeling Justify investment with projected revenue data
Risk management Surprised by downturns or supply problems Anticipate problems and prepare responses in advance
Water management Restrictions imposed reactively when levels are already critical Restrictions triggered 6 months before the threshold is breached
Key Insight

Forecasting is both a risk management tool and a growth enabler. The goal is always to make better decisions, not to produce a perfect number.

The Three Forecasting Horizons

LO1 · Strategic Context

Different business decisions require different time horizons. The horizon determines how much data you need, which method is appropriate, and how accurate you can expect to be.

Horizon Timeframe Typical Decisions Example
Strategic 2 – 5 years Market entry, infrastructure, capacity investment Should we build a second dam? When will water demand exceed current supply?
Operational Weeks – months Production, inventory, workforce planning How much stock to order next quarter? How many staff on shift in July?
Tactical Days – weeks Pricing, promotions, immediate resource deployment Should we run a weekend discount? How much to stock for Easter weekend?
Each horizon requires different methods and has different accuracy expectations. We cover all three across this subject.

Two Approaches to Forecasting

LO1 · Forecasting Methods Overview

Not every forecasting situation has historical data available. The choice of approach depends on what information you have.

Qualitative

Use when: data is scarce, ambiguous, or the future will look very different from the past.

  • Relies on structured expert judgement
  • Useful for new products or markets
  • Cannot be validated statistically
Example: A tech start-up has no sales history. Experts use the Delphi Method to estimate demand for a new software product.
Quantitative

Use when: you have sufficient historical data and the future is expected to follow past patterns.

  • Uses statistical and mathematical models
  • Produces measurable error estimates (Law II)
  • Can be validated and compared objectively
Example: A water authority has 10 years of monthly dam level data. They use SARIMA to forecast the next 5 years.

What is Data-Driven Decision Making?

LO2 · DDDM
Definition

Data-Driven Decision Making (DDDM) means making decisions backed by evidence — not just intuition or observation alone.

Forecasting produces the evidence. But evidence only changes decisions if it is communicated clearly. A great forecast presented poorly will be ignored.

1

Define the problem

What decision needs to be made? Who makes it?

2

Gather data

Collect relevant, reliable historical data

3

Analyse & forecast

Apply appropriate forecasting methods

4

Communicate results

Present clearly with uncertainty and business implications

5

Make the decision

Act on the evidence; monitor and revise as new data arrives

Important Note for Your Assessments

How you communicate results will influence the decisions others make. A number without a business recommendation is not a complete analysis.

What is a Time Series?

LO4 · Time Series Fundamentals
Definition

A time series is a sequence of values recorded at regular time intervals, where the order of observations matters.

The most important property: a time series is correlated with itself. This month's value is related to last month's value. This is called autocorrelation, and it is what makes forecasting possible.

A time series captures three main patterns:

  • Trend — a long-run upward or downward direction
  • Seasonality — a pattern that repeats at regular intervals
  • Noise (remainder) — random fluctuations that cannot be predicted

Decomposing a Time Series

LO4 · Trend, Seasonality, Noise

A useful mental model: every time series is made up of three layers stacked on top of each other. The chart below shows the same airline passenger data broken apart.

Trend
The overall direction of the data — growing, shrinking, or flat. In this data, passenger numbers increased year-on-year.
Seasonality
A repeating pattern within each year — here, peaks in July/August (school holidays) and troughs in winter.
Noise (Remainder)
What is left after trend and seasonality are removed. Ideally this should look random — if it still has a pattern, the model is missing something.

Data Quality: Why It Matters

LO3 · Data Preparation

Your forecasts are only as good as the data behind them. Before fitting any model, you must understand and address data quality issues.

Completeness

No missing values

Time series models need a complete sequence. Missing months must be identified and filled using an appropriate imputation method — not ignored.

Accuracy

Outliers and errors detected

A data entry error or a one-off event (e.g., COVID lockdowns) can distort your model. Outliers need to be identified and handled deliberately.

Consistency

Uniform format and intervals

All observations must be at the same frequency (monthly, weekly, etc.). Mixing annual and monthly observations breaks the time series structure.

Relevance

Right variables for the question

Collecting more data is not always better. Only include variables that have a logical connection to what you are forecasting.

Common Problems You Will Encounter

Irregular time intervals  ·  Structural breaks (e.g., a company merger)  ·  Multiple data sources that do not align  ·  Missing values at the start or end of a series

Week 1 Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Forecasting is a systematic, data-based process of predicting future values — not guessing. Forecasts are always wrong, always need an error estimate, and are less accurate over longer horizons.
  • Businesses need forecasting to manage inventory, staffing, investment, and risk. The three horizons (strategic, operational, tactical) require different approaches.
  • Data-driven decisions require evidence communicated clearly. A forecast without a business recommendation is incomplete.
  • Time-series and panel data are the two data types used in forecasting. Cross-sectional data cannot be used to forecast on its own.
  • Every time series has three components: trend, seasonality, and noise. Understanding these drives every method we study from Week 2 onwards.
  • Data quality must be addressed before modelling — missing values, outliers, and inconsistent intervals must all be handled deliberately.
