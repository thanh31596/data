◆ Question 1 — Descriptive Statistics Profile & Outlier Detection
Building a Complete Salary Profile
Meridian Solutions has expanded to 25 staff. Annual salaries ($000) are listed below. The CFO has asked for a complete descriptive statistics report ahead of the compensation review.
42  44  45  47  48  50  51  52
53  54  55  56  56  57  58  60
62  64  65  68  70  72  75  78  135

Red = suspected outlier (CEO)

Required calculations:

  1. Calculate mean, median, and mode using Excel functions
  2. Calculate Q₁, Q₂, Q₃ and IQR using QUARTILE.INC
  3. Calculate range, variance, and standard deviation
  4. Using the IQR fence method, formally determine whether $135K is a statistical outlier
    Lower fence = Q₁ − 1.5 × IQR  ·  Upper fence = Q₃ + 1.5 × IQR
  5. Based on your results: which measure of centre should HR report to staff — and why?

The IQR fence method — what is it?

Tukey's fences define a principled rule for outliers. Any observation below the lower fence or above the upper fence is classified as a statistical outlier. This is the standard method used by Excel's boxplot feature and most statistical software.

Challenge: part (e)

Don't just say "use the median." Justify your answer quantitatively. Calculate both measures, then compute the trimmed mean (excluding the outlier) and compare all three. Which best represents typical staff pay?

Hint: enter data in a single column

Put each salary in cells A2:A26 with the header "Salary" in A1. All subsequent formulas reference this named range. Sort the column first using Data → Sort to help verify quartile positions manually.

◆ Question 2 — Coefficient of Variation: Department Comparison
Which Team Has More Consistent Pay?
Meridian Solutions has two main teams. The CFO wants to know which team shows greater relative pay dispersion — a signal of inequitable banding structures.

Finance Team — n = 8

55  62  65  70  72  78  85  92

Operations Team — n = 10

42  44  45  47  48  50  51  52  53  54

Required:

  1. For each team, calculate mean and standard deviation in Excel
  2. Calculate CV (%) for each team
  3. Without using CV, a colleague argues "Finance is more variable because its standard deviation is larger." Is this reasoning correct? Explain
  4. Which team should HR investigate for internal pay equity? Why?

The trap in this question

Finance salaries are higher and more spread in absolute terms. But the question is about relative spread — whether the variation is large compared to the team's own mean. CV removes the unit scale and lets you compare apples to apples.

When does CV matter most?

CV is especially important when comparing distributions with very different means — like junior Operations staff vs senior Finance staff — or when comparing variables in entirely different units (e.g., salary vs hours worked).

Pay equity context

A higher CV within a team suggests salaries are not consistently banded. This can indicate ad-hoc salary negotiations, role overlap, or gender/tenure pay gaps. The team with the higher CV warrants deeper investigation.

◆ Question 3 — Geometric Mean & Payroll Projection
What Is Meridian's True Payroll Growth Rate?
Meridian's total payroll budget has changed each year for the past five years. The board needs a single compound annual growth rate (CAGR) to assess payroll sustainability and project future costs.
YearPayroll changeGrowth factor (1 + R)
2020+8.0%1.0800
2021+12.0%1.1200
2022−5.0%0.9500
2023+15.0%1.1500
2024+6.0%1.0600

Required:

  1. Enter the five growth factors in a column. Use GEOMEAN to compute the geometric mean of the growth factors, then subtract 1 to get the CAGR
  2. Separately calculate the arithmetic mean of the five annual rates (not the growth factors)
  3. Current payroll (end of 2024): $1,400,000. Project payroll for end of 2027 using the geometric CAGR. Use a formula — not manual multiplication
  4. Why must the geometric mean be used for this projection, not the arithmetic mean?

Common mistake

Students often apply GEOMEAN directly to the percentage rates (8%, 12%, −5%…). This is wrong — you cannot take the geometric mean of negative numbers. Always convert rates to growth factors (1 + R) first, then subtract 1 from the result.

The projection formula

If CAGR = Rg and current value = P₀, then the value in t years is:

P_t = P₀ × (1 + Rg)^t

In Excel: =P0 * (1 + CAGR)^3 for a 3-year projection.

Hint: verify your CAGR manually

Check: 1.08 × 1.12 × 0.95 × 1.15 × 1.06 = ?
The product of all five growth factors should equal (1 + CAGR)⁵.

Part (d) — Why geometric, not arithmetic?

The arithmetic mean treats each year's rate independently. But payroll compounds — a 15% rise in 2023 applies to a base already reduced 5% in 2022. The geometric mean correctly accounts for this compounding effect. Using the arithmetic rate of 7.2% would project $1,721,900 — overstating the budget by $8,060.

✔ Solution 4B — Sensitivity Analysis & Professional Interpretation
Q4 Continued: Does Employee J Drive the Result?

Sensitivity result: J has minimal influence

Removing J changes r from −0.9953 to −0.9938 — a difference of only 0.0015. This is negligible. The strong negative correlation is not an artefact of a single outlier; it is present throughout all 12 observations. Every point follows the trend closely (r² = 99.1%), confirming the relationship is genuine and consistent.

When would sensitivity matter?

Sensitivity analysis is most important when r is moderate (e.g., 0.6–0.8) and sample sizes are small. If removing one point shifts r by ±0.1 or more, the conclusion depends heavily on that observation. Always test — never assume.

Part (e) — Should HR approve the training budget?

The correlation provides strong statistical support but HR must communicate two important caveats:

  • Correlation ≠ causation: We cannot conclude from r alone that training caused the error reduction. High-ability staff may both choose to train more and be less error-prone
  • Sample size: n = 12 is small. A larger study would strengthen the business case
  • Confounding variables: Role type, years of experience, and software used are not controlled for
  • Correct framing: "Training hours are strongly negatively correlated with errors (r = −0.995, n = 12). This is consistent with training reducing errors, but further evidence is needed before establishing causation."

Required:

  1. In Excel, add a column for m × f (midpoint × frequency). Sum these to get ∑(mf). Compute the approximate mean: x̄ ≈ ∑(mf) / n
  2. Add a column for f × (m − x̄)². Use this to compute the approximate variance and standard deviation
  3. The actual mean from the raw data is $1,847. Compute the percentage approximation error
  4. The last class (5,000–<10,000) is very wide. Explain how this affects the accuracy of your estimates — and what you could do to improve it

Why does this technique matter?

In accounting practice you frequently receive data in aggregated form — ATO income tax statistics by bracket, general ledger balances by value range, insurance claims by severity band. This technique lets you extract meaningful statistics from published summaries without the raw data.

The midpoint assumption

By using the midpoint we assume values are uniformly distributed within each class. This works reasonably well for narrow, equal-width classes. Wide or open-ended classes introduce more error — and unequal class widths bias the midpoint assumption further.

Hint: use absolute cell references

When computing f × (m − x̄)², lock the mean cell: =C2*(B2-$G$2)^2. This lets you copy the formula down without the mean reference shifting.

Part (d) — Wide final class impact

The 5,000–<10,000 class has a width of $5,000 — ten times wider than the first class. Using its midpoint of $7,500 introduces significant error when invoices cluster near one end. Audit implication

A 2.2% approximation error is acceptable for initial analytical review. However, for final audit reporting, request the raw data. Frequency table summaries can mask clustering patterns that matter for materiality assessments.

Use .S for samples (divides by n−1). Use .P only for entire populations. CV = =STDEV.S/AVERAGE * 100.

Outlier Fences

=Q1 − 1.5*IQR [lower]
=Q3 + 1.5*IQR [upper]
=IF(MAX(r)>fence,"YES","NO")

Relationships

=CORREL(x_range, y_range)
=COVARIANCE.S(x, y)
=COVARIANCE.P(x, y)

CORREL always gives Pearson's r. Square it for R² (proportion of variance explained).

Grouped Data

=SUMPRODUCT(m,f)/n
=SUMPRODUCT(f,(m−x̄)^2)/(n−1)
=SQRT(variance)

SUMPRODUCT is the cleanest way to compute ∑(mf) and ∑f(m−x̄)² without adding helper columns.

Sample vs Population — the rule

If data = a sample drawn from a larger group → use .S (n−1 denominator)
If data = the entire population → use .P (N denominator)
