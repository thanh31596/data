What is a Large Language Model?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is a deep neural network trained on vast amounts of text data that can understand, generate, and reason with human language.

Business Analogy: Think of an LLM as a new employee who has read every document, report, and email your industry has ever produced — but has never actually worked in your company. It has extraordinary general knowledge but needs context about your specific business.

LLMs are built on the Transformer architecture (introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need"), which enables them to process language by focusing on the relationships between all words simultaneously rather than reading sequentially.

Tokens: The Currency of LLMs

LLMs do not read words — they read tokens. Understanding tokens is essential for managing cost and performance.

How Tokenisation Works

A token is a piece of text — roughly ¾ of a word on average. LLMs break all input and output into tokens before processing.

Example: "Customer churn prediction model"

Customer ch urn predict ion model

4 words → 6 tokens

Why This Matters for Business

Every API call is priced per token. A 10-page report ≈ 3,000 tokens. Understanding this helps you estimate costs and optimise your prompts for efficiency.

Embeddings: How LLMs Understand Meaning

Embeddings convert text into numerical vectors that capture semantic meaning — similar meanings produce similar numbers.

An embedding is a list of numbers (a vector) that represents the meaning of a piece of text. Words or sentences with similar meanings have vectors that are close together in this numerical space.

Why Embeddings Matter for Business

Semantic Search

Search by meaning rather than exact keywords. A customer searching "can't log in" finds results about "authentication failure" and "password reset."

Recommendation Systems

Find similar products, documents, or customer profiles by comparing their embedding vectors.

Document Retrieval (RAG)

The foundation of RAG — embed your business documents and retrieve the most relevant ones to answer a question. We will build this today.

Temperature: Controlling LLM Creativity

Temperature is a parameter that controls how "creative" or "deterministic" the model's output is.

0.0
Deterministic
"Based on Q3 data, customer churn increased by 12.3% compared to Q2, primarily driven by pricing changes in the premium tier."

Use for: Financial reports, compliance documents, data summaries, factual Q&A

0.7
Balanced
"Our Q3 churn numbers tell an interesting story — the 12.3% increase wasn't uniform. Premium customers are voting with their feet, and the pricing changes appear to be the catalyst."

Use for: Business emails, strategic recommendations, general analysis

1.5
Creative
"Picture a leaky bucket — that's our premium tier right now. Every price hike punches a new hole, and Q3's 12.3% churn spike is the water pouring out. Time to rethink the vessel entirely."

Use for: Marketing copy, brainstorming sessions, creative campaigns

Business Takeaway

Choosing the right temperature is a business decision, not a technical one. The same LLM becomes a different tool depending on this single parameter.

Context Windows: The LLM's Working Memory

The context window determines how much text an LLM can "see" at once — both your input and its output must fit within this window.

Every LLM has a maximum context window measured in tokens. Think of it as the model's desk — everything it needs to read and write must fit on this desk simultaneously.

Context windows have grown 500x in just four years, fundamentally changing what LLMs can process in a single request.

Business Implications

Larger window = more context
You can feed an entire contract, policy manual, or dataset into a single prompt for analysis.

Larger window = higher cost
You pay for every token in the context window. Processing a 100-page document costs significantly more than a 1-page query.

Window size ≠ understanding
LLMs perform best on information near the beginning and end of the context. Critical details buried in the middle may be missed — this is known as the "lost in the middle" problem.

This is why we need RAG: Rather than stuffing everything into the context window, RAG retrieves only the most relevant information. More effective, and far cheaper.

The Hallucination Problem

LLMs sometimes generate confident, fluent text that is factually incorrect — this is the central challenge for business deployment.

WITHOUT GROUNDING

"MobTel's Premium Plus plan includes unlimited international calls to 45 countries and a free Netflix subscription."

This sounds plausible but is entirely fabricated — the LLM has no access to MobTel's actual plan details.

WITH RAG GROUNDING

"According to MobTel's current plan documentation, the Premium Plus plan includes unlimited domestic calls and 500 international minutes to 12 countries. No streaming subscriptions are included."

Grounded in retrieved company documents — accurate and verifiable.

Why Do LLMs Hallucinate?

LLMs are trained to predict the most probable next token — not to verify facts. They generate text that sounds right based on patterns in training data, even when the specific claim has no basis.

3–27%
Hallucination rates across models (Vectara Hallucination Index, 2024)

Business Risk: A hallucinating customer service bot could promise non-existent discounts. A hallucinating compliance tool could cite non-existent regulations. This is why frameworks like RAG exist — to ground LLM responses in verified, authoritative sources.

Choosing the Right AI Framework

Each framework solves a different problem. The right choice depends on your business context, not the technology's sophistication.

Framework What It Does Best For Cost Setup Time Data Needed
Prompt Engineering Crafting instructions that guide the LLM's behaviour General tasks, prototyping, ad hoc analysis Low Minutes None
RAG Retrieving relevant documents to ground LLM responses Knowledge bases, customer support, document Q&A Medium Days Your documents
Fine-Tuning Retraining the model on your specific data Domain language, brand voice, specialised classification High Weeks Thousands of examples
Agentic AI LLM orchestrates tools and makes decisions autonomously Multi-step workflows, automated processes Variable Weeks Tools + APIs
Practical Rule of Thumb

Start with prompt engineering. If the model doesn't have the right knowledge, add RAG. If it doesn't speak the right language or style, consider fine-tuning. If it needs to take actions, build an agent. These are not mutually exclusive — production systems often combine multiple frameworks.

Framework 1: Prompt Engineering

The art of instructing an LLM through carefully designed input text

Prompt engineering requires no infrastructure, no training data, and no code beyond the API call itself. It is the fastest, cheapest, and most accessible AI framework — and often sufficient for a wide range of business tasks.

Key Techniques

Role Assignment: "You are a senior financial analyst specialising in telecommunications..."

Few-Shot Examples: Provide 2–3 examples of the desired output format before the actual request.

Chain of Thought: "Explain your reasoning step by step before providing the final answer."

Structured Output: "Respond in JSON format with keys: sentiment, confidence, reasoning."

When Prompt Engineering Is Enough

Use prompt engineering alone when the task relies on general knowledge, the output quality is acceptable with instruction alone, and you do not need the model to reference proprietary data.

Limitations

Prompt engineering cannot give the model knowledge it does not already have. If your business data is proprietary, recent, or specialised, the model will either hallucinate or acknowledge ignorance. This is precisely where RAG becomes essential.

Framework 2: Fine-Tuning

Retraining a pre-trained LLM on your specific data to alter its behaviour permanently

Fine-tuning modifies the model's internal weights using your own dataset. The result is a model that "speaks your language" — it has absorbed your domain vocabulary, writing style, or classification patterns.

When to Fine-Tune

Fine-tuning makes sense when you need the model to consistently adopt a specific tone, style, or classification behaviour — and you have sufficient labelled examples (typically 1,000+).

Fine-Tuning vs RAG: A Common Confusion

Fine-tuning changes HOW the model responds
It alters the model's language patterns, style, and behaviour. Think of it as teaching the model a new dialect.

RAG changes WHAT the model knows
It provides the model with specific information at query time. Think of it as giving the model a reference book to consult.

Business Example

A legal firm wants their AI to draft contracts. Fine-tuning teaches it to write in legal prose style. RAG ensures it references the correct clauses from their template library. In practice, they likely need both.

Important: Fine-tuning is expensive, requires ML expertise, and the model needs retraining whenever your data changes. For most business analytics use cases, RAG or prompt engineering will be the more practical choice.

Framework 3: Agentic AI

When the LLM doesn't just answer questions — it takes actions

An AI agent is an LLM that can reason about a task, decide which tools to use, execute actions, and evaluate the results — repeating this cycle until the task is complete.

Business Analogy: A chatbot is like a receptionist who can only answer questions. An AI agent is like a junior analyst who can look things up, run calculations, draft reports, and send emails — all based on a single instruction from you.

The Agent Loop

Think
Reason about task
→
Act
Call a tool
→
Observe
Read the result
→
Repeat
Until done

What Can an Agent Do?

An agent's capabilities are defined by the tools you give it access to. Common business tools include:

Database Queries — look up customer records, sales data, inventory levels

API Calls — trigger workflows, send notifications, update CRM systems

Calculations — run financial models, statistical analysis, forecasts

Document Retrieval (RAG) — search knowledge bases for relevant context

Code Execution — write and run Python scripts to analyse data

Note: RAG is often a tool within an agent, not an alternative to it. A sophisticated business AI might use RAG for knowledge, function calling for actions, and prompt engineering for reasoning — all orchestrated by an agent.

What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation?

RAG combines the retrieval capabilities of a search engine with the generation capabilities of an LLM

RAG is a framework that retrieves relevant documents from a knowledge base and augments the LLM's prompt with this context before generating a response.

Analogy: Imagine you are writing an exam. Without RAG, you must answer entirely from memory — you might remember things incorrectly. With RAG, you are allowed to bring a reference book and look up the exact information before writing your answer. Your response is more accurate because it is grounded in source material.

RAG was introduced by Lewis et al. (2020) at Facebook AI Research and has since become the dominant pattern for enterprise LLM deployment.

The Three Steps of RAG

1. Embed — Convert your documents into numerical vectors (embeddings) and store them in a vector database.

2. Retrieve — When a user asks a question, convert it to a vector and find the most similar document chunks.

3. Generate — Feed the retrieved documents + the user's question to the LLM, which generates a grounded answer.

How Similarity Search Works

Finding the most relevant documents by measuring distance between embedding vectors

When a user asks a question, the system converts it into a vector and measures its "distance" from every document vector in the database. The closest vectors represent the most semantically relevant documents.

Key Concepts

Cosine Similarity

The most common distance metric. Measures the angle between two vectors — a value of 1.0 means identical meaning, 0.0 means completely unrelated. Our notebook will use this.

Top-K Retrieval

You choose how many documents to retrieve (K). Typical values are K=3 to K=5. Too few may miss relevant context; too many may overwhelm the LLM with irrelevant information.

Chunking Strategy

Documents are split into smaller chunks before embedding (typically 200–500 words). The chunk size affects retrieval precision — too large and you get noise, too small and you lose context.

Evaluating a RAG System

A RAG system can fail at two stages — retrieval and generation. Both must be measured.

Retrieval Quality

Did the system find the right documents?

Precision: Of the documents retrieved, how many were actually relevant?

Recall: Of all the relevant documents that exist, how many did we retrieve?

Relevance Score: How closely do the retrieved chunks match the query semantically?

Generation Quality

Did the LLM use the documents correctly?

Faithfulness: Is the generated answer supported by the retrieved documents? (No hallucination)

Completeness: Did the answer address all parts of the user's question?

Conciseness: Is the answer focused and free of irrelevant information from the retrieved context?

Why Both Matter

A perfect retrieval system paired with a poor generator will waste good context. A perfect generator paired with poor retrieval will generate beautiful answers based on the wrong information. Both stages must perform well for the system to be trustworthy.
