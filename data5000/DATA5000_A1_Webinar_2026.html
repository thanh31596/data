Section 1

The Business Scenario

Before you touch the notebook, you need to understand who you are and what you have been asked to deliver.

1 — The Business Scenario

CloudPulse: Your Company

The Situation

You are a Senior Business Analyst at CloudPulse, a B2B SaaS company providing cloud-based analytics dashboards to mid-market and enterprise clients. Over the past 18 months, the monthly churn rate has been rising steadily. The Customer Success team has piloted a dedicated account management tier — ProSupport — but the executive board wants rigorous evidence before funding a full rollout.

Your job is to deliver that evidence in a professional business report.

The Dataset

  • 2,400 B2B customer records
  • 13 features: tenure, MRR, NPS score, support tickets, feature adoption, login frequency, contract type, integrations, onboarding score, CS engagement, segment, plan, and ProSupport enrolment
  • Binary outcome: churned (1) or stayed (0)

The Three Customer Segments

  • SMB — small and medium businesses (45% of base)
  • Mid-Market — growing companies (38%)
  • Enterprise — large accounts (17%)

Four subscription plans: Starter, Growth, Pro, Enterprise.

1 — The Business Scenario

Your Deliverable: A Business Report, Not a Technical Write-Up

Who You Are Writing For

The executive board at CloudPulse. They understand revenue, customer lifetime value, and programme cost. They do not understand SHAP values, AUC scores, or gradient boosting. Your job is to translate your analysis into language they can act on.

What an Executive Needs

  • "Our churn rate in the SMB segment is X% — the highest in the business"
  • "The ProSupport programme causally reduces churn by X percentage points, after controlling for the fact that better customers self-selected into it"
  • "Targeting the X% of customers identified by the policy tree would prevent approximately Y churns at a cost of $Z — versus $W per churn prevented by calling the top 20% at-risk customers"

What an Executive Does Not Need

  • "We used LightGBM with a learning rate of 0.04 and 31 num_leaves"
  • "The SHAP library computes Shapley values based on cooperative game theory"
  • "We applied LinearDML from the EconML library with 4-fold cross-fitting"

Technical details belong in a methods appendix, not the body of the report.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1A

Data Exploration & Preparation

What the Code Does

  • Loads 2,400 CloudPulse customer records
  • Generates six exploratory charts: churn by segment, plan, feature adoption, support tickets, ProSupport comparison, and tenure
  • Prepares the feature matrix for machine learning

Blank 1 in This Phase

TARGET = ___

You need to name the column you are predicting. Read the scenario. What outcome are you trying to model?

What to Look For in the Charts

  • Which segment has churn above the population average?
  • How does feature adoption differ between churned and retained customers?
  • Does the tenure chart show an early-lifecycle churn problem?
  • The ProSupport raw comparison looks promising — but note the caveat printed on the chart. This will become important in Phase 1D.

For Your Report

Frame the problem in revenue terms. "The SMB segment has a churn rate of X%" is a starting point. "The SMB segment represents Y% of our customer base and accounts for approximately $Z in at-risk monthly revenue" is what earns marks.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1B

Predictive Modelling — LightGBM

What the Code Does

  • Trains a LightGBM gradient-boosted classifier on 75% of the data
  • Evaluates it on the held-out 25% using AUC, precision, recall, and a confusion matrix
  • Produces a predicted churn probability for every customer
  • Ranks customers by risk score and identifies the highest-risk accounts

Blank 2 in This Phase

NUM_ROUNDS = ___

Set the maximum number of boosting rounds. Any value between 200 and 600 is correct — early stopping finds the optimum automatically. Do not overthink this one.

What AUC Means in Plain Language

AUC is the probability that the model ranks a randomly chosen churner above a randomly chosen stayer. An AUC of 0.85 means the model gets this ranking correct 85% of the time. A coin flip would score 0.50.

In your report: connect AUC to business confidence. Can CloudPulse trust this model to prioritise outreach?

The Confusion Matrix Trade-off

A false negative (predicting someone stays when they actually churn) costs CloudPulse a lost customer and their full lifetime value. A false positive (flagging someone who would have stayed anyway) costs one unnecessary ProSupport call. In a retention context, false negatives are far more expensive. Discuss this in your report.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1C

Explainable AI — SHAP

Three Visualisations You Will Generate

  • Feature importance bar chart — ranks all 12 features by mean absolute SHAP value. This is your "top 5 churn drivers" list.
  • Beeswarm plot — shows the direction of each feature's effect. Red dots = high feature value; blue = low. Right = pushes churn up; left = pushes it down.
  • Waterfall plot — deconstructs one specific customer's churn probability step by step, from the population average to the final prediction.

The Single Most Important Distinction in This Assessment

SHAP shows what the model is using to make predictions. It shows correlation.

It does not show what would happen if you changed a feature.

Wrong: "If we increase feature adoption, customers will stay."

Right: "Low feature adoption is strongly associated with higher churn risk."

Whether changing adoption causes customers to stay is tested in Phase 1D. This distinction alone is worth significant marks.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1D

Causal Analysis — Three Tools, Three Questions

Why Prediction Is Not Enough

LightGBM tells us who will churn. SHAP tells us which features are correlated with that prediction. But the board's question is different: if we invest in ProSupport, will customers actually stay? That requires causal inference — not prediction.

Tool 1 — LinearDML

Question: Does ProSupport causally reduce churn for individual customers?

Output: ATE (average effect) + CATE (personalised effects)

Level: Individual customer

Tool 2 — Synthetic Control (SCM)

Question: What would the ProSupport cohort's churn rate have been without the programme?

Output: Monthly gap chart showing effect building over time

Level: Cohort aggregate

Tool 3 — Policy Tree

Question: Which customers should we enrol in ProSupport next quarter?

Output: Simple targeting rule + strategy comparison table

Level: Operational decision

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1D

Understanding Selection Bias — Why the Raw Comparison Misleads

What the Data Shows (Before DML)

Customers enrolled in ProSupport have a lower raw churn rate than those not enrolled. A naive analyst concludes: "ProSupport works — it reduces churn by X%." But this comparison is biased.

Why the Comparison Is Biased

Enrolled customers were not randomly assigned. They were more likely to be high-MRR, high-NPS, high-CS-engagement customers to begin with — precisely the customers who were least likely to churn regardless of the programme.

The raw difference is partly the programme's effect and partly the pre-existing quality of the enrolled customer base.

What DML Does About It

DML residualises out the confounders — MRR, NPS, CS engagement, contract type — from both the treatment assignment and the outcome. What remains is only the variation in ProSupport enrolment that is unrelated to those confounders. That is what it regresses on churn to get the clean causal effect.

The DML estimate will be smaller in magnitude than the naive estimate. This is expected — it is more honest.

In your report: explain that you found selection bias in the raw comparison, that DML corrected for it, and that the corrected estimate is your basis for the recommendation. Do not use the naive figure as your headline result.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1D

Synthetic Control: Evaluating the Programme Over Time

The Question SCM Answers

DML tells us the individual-level average effect. SCM asks: for the ProSupport cohort as a group, what would their monthly churn rate trajectory have looked like if the programme had never launched?

How It Works

  • Builds a synthetic twin of the ProSupport cohort using a weighted blend of non-enrolled customer strata (Starter, Growth, Pro cohorts)
  • The weights are chosen so the synthetic cohort matches the real cohort's churn trajectory before the programme launched
  • After launch, the gap between real and synthetic is the programme's causal effect on the cohort

What to Look For in the Chart

  • Pre-period: the two lines should overlap closely — this validates the synthetic control
  • Post-period: does a gap open between real and synthetic? Does it grow over time?
  • RMSPE: the pre-period fit statistic. Below 0.5pp = credible. Above 1pp = treat with caution.

A widening gap over time suggests the programme's benefit compounds — ProSupport relationships deepen, and customers embed the product more thoroughly the longer they stay.

2 — The Four Phases

Phase 1D

Policy Tree: From 2,400 CATEs to One Targeting Rule

Churn Prediction Model (LightGBM)

Trained on: Historical labels — did this customer churn (1) or not (0)?

Output: "This customer has a 78% probability of churning"

Answers: Who will churn?

Limitation: A customer who has decided to leave will have a high churn score — but will not respond to ProSupport.

Policy Tree (EconML)

Trained on: Individual CATE estimates — how much does ProSupport change this customer's churn probability?

Output: "Enrol this customer — expected churn reduction is 12pp"

Answers: Who will respond?

Strength: Correctly deprioritises high-risk customers who will not respond, saving budget for those where the intervention changes the outcome.

Blank 5 — The Most Conceptually Important One

pt.fit(___, ___) — the PolicyTree maximises reward. Your CATEs are negative (ProSupport reduces churn probability). If you pass cates directly, the tree will identify customers where ProSupport increases churn — the opposite of what you want. Think carefully about the sign.

3 — Marking Expectations

What Each Grade Band Looks Like

Fail

Numbers cited but not interpreted. Cannot distinguish SHAP from causal evidence. Recommendations generic or absent.

Pass

Describes outputs. Mentions key metrics. Recommendations present but vague. Limited connection between sections.

Credit

Interprets most outputs correctly. SHAP correlation language mostly right. Recommendations linked to findings but not financially quantified.

Distinction

Clear correlation/causation separation. DML and SCM results interpreted with context. Recommendations cite specific numbers and explain the targeting trade-off.

Coherent argument across all four phases. SHAP findings generate causal hypotheses tested by DML/SCM. Policy tree recommendation includes financial ROI and validation proposal.

The HD Difference in One Sentence

A Credit report describes what each tool produced. A High Distinction report builds a single argument that uses each tool as a stepping stone to the next, arriving at a recommendation that could not have been written without the full causal analysis.

3 — Marking Expectations

Recommendations — The Highest-Weighted Section (5 Marks)

What Will Not Earn a Pass Here

"CloudPulse should focus on improving customer satisfaction and product adoption to reduce churn."

This is not a recommendation. It is a restatement of the problem with no evidence base and no specificity.

What a Strong Recommendation Looks Like

  • Name the target segment: "Based on the CATE analysis, the policy tree recommends enrolling X customers, predominantly from the [segment] segment and [plan] plan tier, where the ProSupport effect is strongest."
  • Quantify the expected outcome: "At an estimated cost of $Y per enrolment, targeted rollout would prevent approximately Z churns — compared to W churns prevented by calling the top 20% highest-risk customers at a cost of $V per churn."
  • Explain the targeting logic: "This approach is more efficient because it targets treatment response, not churn risk — the customers most likely to churn are not necessarily the ones most likely to respond to ProSupport."
  • Propose validation: "We recommend a held-out A/B group in the rollout to validate the DML causal estimate before a full national expansion."

4 — Common Mistakes

Mistakes That Cost the Most Marks

MistakeWhy It Costs MarksHow to Avoid It
Treating SHAP as causal Shows fundamental misunderstanding of the tool's purpose — entire Phase 1C section affected Use "associated with," "predicts," or "correlated with" for SHAP findings. Reserve causal language for DML and SCM.
Reporting numbers without interpreting them "AUC = 0.85" earns zero marks. Interpreting what it means for the business earns full marks. For every number you cite, ask yourself: what does this mean for CloudPulse? What can they do with this?
Using the naive ProSupport comparison as the headline result Ignores the entire point of Phase 1D — the naive estimate is the wrong number Use the DML ATE as your headline causal finding. Note the naive comparison only to show why it is biased.
Generic recommendations Cannot earn a passing mark for the Recommendations criterion regardless of the quality of analysis sections Every recommendation must cite a specific number from your analysis and specify which customers to target.

4 — Common Mistakes

How to Structure the Report

Recommended Structure

  1. Business Problem & Data Overview (~250 words)
    The company, the problem, the data, the initial findings. Anchor it in churn rate and revenue at risk.
  2. Predictive Analytics Findings (~400 words)
    LightGBM performance, what the model tells us, who is most at risk.
  3. Explainable AI Insights (~200 words)
    Top 5 SHAP drivers, directions, one customer deep-dive.
  4. Causal Analysis & Treatment Effects (~150 words)
    ATE, SCM finding, CATE heterogeneity.
  5. Recommendations (~250 words)
    Who to target, expected outcome, cost comparison, validation proposal.

How the Sections Connect

Section 1 asks: what is the problem?

Section 2 asks: can we predict who will churn?

Section 3 asks: what is driving those predictions?

Section 4 asks: can we actually change the outcome?

Section 5 asks: what should we do?

If your report can be read as a flow where each section answers a question raised by the previous one, you are on track for a Distinction or above.
