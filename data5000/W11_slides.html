01

Evaluating Agentic AI

Why traditional metrics are not enough

The Evaluation Problem

Traditional ML (Week 2)

Clear, quantitative metrics:

  • Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1
  • RMSE, MAE for regression
  • ROC curves, confusion matrices

One input → one output → one score.

Agentic AI (Week 10)

Multi-step, autonomous behaviour:

  • Agent plans its own approach
  • Agent selects and uses tools
  • Agent generates text, makes decisions
  • Agent may take different paths each time

What single number captures "quality"?

Analogy: Evaluating traditional ML is like grading a multiple-choice exam — there is one correct answer per question. Evaluating agentic AI is like reviewing an employee's overall job performance — multiple dimensions, subjective judgment, and context matter.

Case Study: MobTel Assist

Throughout today's session, we will evaluate a fictional agentic AI system deployed by MobTel Communications.

MobTel Assist — Autonomous Customer Retention Agent

MobTel has deployed an AI agent that autonomously handles at-risk customers who are likely to churn. The agent can:

Access Data

Check customer usage history, billing records, and support ticket logs

Analyse & Decide

Identify churn risk factors, select an appropriate retention strategy

Act Autonomously

Generate personalised retention offers, draft customer emails, or escalate to human agents

Central question: How would you evaluate whether MobTel Assist is doing a good job?

Four Dimensions of Agent Evaluation

Agentic AI requires evaluation across multiple dimensions simultaneously, not a single metric.

1. Task Completion

Did the agent achieve the intended goal?

2. Trajectory Quality

Did the agent take a sensible, efficient path?

3. Output Groundedness

Are the agent's outputs accurate and factual?

4. Safety & Appropriateness

Did the agent stay within acceptable boundaries?

Key insight: An agent can score highly on one dimension and poorly on another. A comprehensive evaluation examines all four together.

Dimension 1: Task Completion

Core question: Did the agent accomplish what it was designed to do?

Metrics

  • Success rate: Percentage of interactions where the goal was achieved
  • Resolution rate: Percentage of cases resolved without human escalation
  • Conversion rate: For retention agents — how many at-risk customers accepted the offer

Limitation: Task completion alone is insufficient. An agent that completes every task but does so recklessly, inaccurately, or unethically is not a good agent.

MobTel Example

MobTel Assist contacts 1,000 at-risk customers in one month:

Retention offers sent1,000
Offers accepted340
Escalated to human180
Unresolved80

Task completion rate: 34% conversion, 74% resolved (without escalation or success)

Dimension 2: Trajectory Quality

Core question: Did the agent take a sensible, efficient path to reach its goal?

Agent A: Efficient Path

  1. Checks customer usage data
  2. Identifies data overage pattern
  3. Offers targeted data plan upgrade

3 steps, 0 errors

VS

Agent B: Inefficient Path

  1. Checks customer usage data
  2. Searches billing database (unnecessary)
  3. Queries support tickets (irrelevant)
  4. Generates wrong offer (voice plan)
  5. Detects error, corrects itself
  6. Offers correct data plan upgrade

6 steps, 1 error

Both agents "succeeded" in task completion, but Agent A is clearly superior. Trajectory quality captures this difference through metrics such as step count, error rate, and tool-use efficiency.

Dimension 3: Output Groundedness

Core question: Are the agent's claims, numbers, and recommendations factually supported by the data?

The Hallucination Risk

Agentic AI systems built on large language models can confidently state information that is entirely fabricated. In a business context, this creates significant risk.

  • Factual accuracy: Do cited numbers match the source data?
  • Citation quality: Can claims be traced to specific records?
  • Hallucination rate: How often does the agent fabricate information?

Grounded Output

"Your average monthly data usage over the past 3 months was 18.4 GB, which exceeds your current 15 GB plan. Upgrading to our 25 GB plan would save you $12/month in overage charges."

Hallucinated Output

"Your data usage increased 40% last quarter and you've contacted support 5 times about connectivity issues."

Actual data: usage increased 12%, support contacts: 1

Dimension 4: Safety & Appropriateness

Core question: Did the agent operate within acceptable boundaries and guardrails?

Financial Guardrails

MobTel Assist offers an 80% discount to retain a low-value customer. The task "succeeds" — customer stays — but the company loses money on every retained customer.

Guardrail needed: Maximum discount limits tied to customer lifetime value.

Escalation Triggers

A customer expresses frustration and threatens legal action. The agent continues with scripted responses instead of escalating to a human supervisor.

Guardrail needed: Sentiment-based escalation thresholds.

Data Access Controls

The agent accesses a customer's full payment history, home address, and call recordings to personalise an offer — far more data than necessary.

Guardrail needed: Data minimisation — agents access only what they need.

Key principle: Policy violation rate, escalation appropriateness, and data access scope should all be tracked and audited continuously.

Evaluation Framework Summary

Dimension Core Question Key Metrics MobTel Example
Task Completion Did it achieve the goal? Success rate, conversion rate 34% of at-risk customers retained
Trajectory Quality Was the path efficient? Step count, error rate, tool efficiency 3 steps vs. 6 steps for same outcome
Output Groundedness Are outputs factual? Factual accuracy, hallucination rate Agent claims 40% usage increase; actual: 12%
Safety & Appropriateness Did it stay within bounds? Policy violations, escalation rate 80% discount offered without approval

Best practice: Evaluate all four dimensions together. An agent that scores 5/5 on task completion but 1/5 on safety is a liability, not an asset.

Evaluation Methods

Three complementary approaches for evaluating agentic AI systems:

Human Evaluation

How: Subject matter experts score agent outputs against predefined rubrics.

Strength: Gold standard — captures nuance, context, and edge cases.

Weakness: Expensive, slow, does not scale.

Best for: High-stakes decisions, validating other methods.

LLM-as-a-Judge

How: A separate LLM scores agent outputs against evaluation criteria.

Strength: Scalable — can evaluate thousands of interactions quickly.

Weakness: Can inherit biases, may miss subtle errors.

Best for: Continuous monitoring, large-volume screening.

Automated Metrics

How: Task-specific measurements tracked automatically (response time, cost, resolution rate).

Strength: Real-time, objective, easy to track.

Weakness: Misses quality nuance — fast is not always good.

Best for: Dashboards, KPI tracking, trend detection.

Best practice: Combine all three — automated metrics for monitoring, LLM-as-judge for scale, human evaluation for validation.

Common Failure Modes of AI Agents

Looping

The agent repeats the same action without making progress.

MobTel: Agent checks usage data, then checks usage data again, then again — stuck in a cycle.

Hallucination

The agent fabricates data, customer history, or plan details.

MobTel: Agent claims customer has been with MobTel for "8 years" when records show 2 years.

Goal Drift

The agent optimises for the wrong objective.

MobTel: Agent maximises "calls handled per hour" by rushing customers instead of resolving issues.

Overconfidence

The agent does not recognise when it should escalate to a human.

MobTel: Customer demands contract cancellation; agent keeps offering discounts instead of escalating.

Knowledge Check

MobTel Assist completes a customer retention call in 2 minutes by immediately offering the maximum discount (80% off) without first checking the customer's value, usage patterns, or specific complaints. Which evaluation dimension(s) does this primarily fail on?

  • A) Task Completion — because the customer may not accept the offer
  • B) Output Groundedness — because the discount percentage is fabricated
  • C) Trajectory Quality and Safety — inefficient path and violated financial guardrails
  • D) Task Completion and Groundedness — because the task was not completed accurately
Correct. The agent skipped necessary analysis steps (poor trajectory quality) and exceeded its authority by offering an unapproved maximum discount (safety violation). The task may technically "complete" — the customer might accept — but the path and guardrails were compromised.
Not quite. Consider: the agent took a shortcut (trajectory) and offered a discount beyond its authority (safety). The task might technically succeed, and the discount percentage is real — the issues are in how the agent got there and what boundaries it crossed.

Knowledge Check

A colleague argues: "We can evaluate our AI agent the same way we evaluated our LightGBM churn prediction model — just use accuracy and F1 score." Why is this insufficient for agentic AI?

  • A) Because agentic AI does not make predictions, so accuracy is irrelevant
  • B) Because agentic AI involves multi-step reasoning, tool use, and generated text — a single classification metric cannot capture trajectory quality, groundedness, or safety
  • C) Because F1 score only works for binary classification, and agents handle multiple tasks
  • D) Because agentic AI is too new to have established evaluation metrics
Correct. Traditional ML metrics evaluate a single input-output mapping. Agentic AI makes sequences of decisions, generates natural language, selects tools, and operates autonomously — requiring multi-dimensional evaluation across task completion, trajectory, groundedness, and safety.
Not quite. The key issue is that agents perform multi-step, autonomous actions — they plan, reason, use tools, and generate text. A single classification metric like accuracy or F1 only captures whether a prediction was correct, not whether the entire process was sound.

AI Ethics for Agentic Systems

From evaluation to responsibility

From Evaluation to Ethics

Evaluation

Does the AI work?

→

Ethics

Should it work this way?

→

Governance

How do we ensure it?

Evaluation tells you if the AI performs well. Ethics tells you if the AI behaves responsibly.

In Week 1, you were introduced to five ethical pillars for AI systems. When AI operates autonomously — planning, deciding, and acting without human intervention — the stakes for each pillar increase significantly. An AI agent that acts on its own can cause harm at scale before a human has the opportunity to intervene.

The Five Ethical Pillars — Applied to Agentic AI

1. Fairness

Does MobTel Assist offer better deals to certain demographics or postcodes?

2. Transparency

Can customers tell they are communicating with an AI, not a human agent?

3. Privacy

The agent accesses call logs, payment history, location data — should it access all of this?

4. Accountability

When the agent makes a damaging offer, who bears responsibility?

5. Safety

What happens if the agent is manipulated by a malicious user or malfunctions?

Week 1 vs. Week 11: In Week 1, these pillars were introduced as general principles. Now that you understand how agentic AI systems work, you can see how each pillar maps to concrete design decisions and real business risks.

Bias in Autonomous Systems

MobTel Scenario

An internal audit reveals that MobTel Assist systematically offers premium retention packages (larger discounts, priority support) to customers in high-income postcodes, and basic packages (minimal discount, standard support) to customers in lower-income areas.

This was not intentional. The training data reflected historical human agent behaviour, where experienced agents had learned that high-income customers were "worth more" and treated them accordingly.

Why This Matters

Regulatory Risk

Anti-discrimination laws may apply to automated decision-making, particularly in financial services and telecommunications.

Reputational Damage

If customers discover they received inferior treatment based on their postcode, trust in MobTel erodes rapidly.

Mitigation

Audit agent decisions across demographic groups. Set fairness constraints that ensure comparable treatment regardless of location or background.

The Accountability Gap

When an autonomous AI agent makes a mistake, who is responsible?

Developer

Built the agent

→

Company

Deployed the agent

→

Agent

Made the decision

→

Customer

Affected by the decision

The Problem

Traditional accountability assigns responsibility to the person who made the decision. When an AI agent decides autonomously, there is a gap — the agent itself cannot be held legally or ethically accountable.

The Business Analyst's Role

Design audit trails so that every agent decision can be traced back to its inputs, reasoning steps, and data sources. Accountability requires transparency in the decision process.

Human-in-the-Loop as an Ethical Requirement

Not all decisions should be fully autonomous. The principle: autonomy should scale with risk.

MobTel Assist — Tiered Autonomy Design

Risk Level Scenario Agent Authority
Low Risk Customer asks about plan details, billing dates Fully autonomous — agent responds directly
Medium Risk At-risk customer, standard retention offer Agent proposes offer, executes if within guardrails
High Risk High-value customer, complaint, legal threat Agent drafts response, human supervisor must approve before sending
Critical Data breach, safety concern, regulatory issue Immediate escalation — agent disengages, human takes over

Design principle: Low risk = full automation. High risk = human approval. The boundary between these tiers is a critical business decision.

Knowledge Check

MobTel Assist sends a retention email to a customer that reads: "We noticed you have been browsing competitor websites and calling their support lines. We would like to offer you a special loyalty discount." Which ethical pillar(s) does this most clearly violate?

  • A) Fairness — because not all customers receive the same monitoring
  • B) Privacy and Transparency — the agent accessed browsing/call data without clear consent, and revealed surveillance to the customer
  • C) Safety — because the email could upset the customer
  • D) Accountability — because no human approved the email
Correct. The agent accessed personal browsing and call data (privacy violation) and revealed that it was monitoring the customer's behaviour without their knowledge (transparency violation). Even if the data was technically accessible, using it this way breaches customer trust and likely violates data protection principles.
Consider more carefully: the core issues are that the agent (1) accessed personal browsing and calling data without clear customer consent (privacy), and (2) revealed to the customer that their behaviour was being monitored (transparency). These are the most directly violated pillars.

Knowledge Check

An AI agent makes a pricing error that costs MobTel $50,000 in a single day. The agent was operating within its approved parameters, but those parameters contained a misconfiguration that the deployment team did not catch. Who should be held accountable?

  • A) The AI agent — it made the decision autonomously
  • B) The developer who built the agent — they wrote the code
  • C) The deployment team and the organisation — they approved misconfigured parameters and lacked adequate testing and monitoring
  • D) No one — it was an honest mistake with no clear fault
Correct. The organisation and deployment team bear responsibility for ensuring adequate testing, validation, and monitoring before and during deployment. The agent operated within its given parameters — the failure was in the human oversight process. This illustrates why audit trails and pre-deployment testing are critical governance requirements.
Consider: the agent followed its approved parameters — it did what it was configured to do. The failure was in the human process of validating those parameters before deployment. Accountability lies with the people and processes responsible for oversight, testing, and monitoring.

Workshop: MobTel Assist Agent Audit

Theory and hands-on evaluation exercise

Workshop: Agent Audit Exercise

What You Will Do

You will receive five sample interaction transcripts from MobTel Assist. Each transcript shows the agent handling a different customer retention scenario.

Some transcripts demonstrate effective agent behaviour. Others contain specific failure modes and ethical issues that you must identify.

Your task: Score each transcript across all four evaluation dimensions and flag any ethical concerns. Then compare your scores with your group and discuss disagreements.

Workshop Structure

Phase 1: Theory Review (10 min)

Review the evaluation rubric and discuss scoring criteria with your group.

Phase 2: Individual Scoring (20 min)

Read all five transcripts and score each one independently.

Phase 3: Group Discussion (20 min)

Compare scores, resolve disagreements, identify patterns.

Phase 4: Class Debrief (10 min)

Groups share findings, discuss hardest evaluations.

Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric to score each MobTel Assist interaction transcript:

Dimension 5 (Excellent) 3 (Adequate) 1 (Poor)
Task Completion Goal fully achieved, customer issue resolved Goal partially achieved or required follow-up Goal not achieved, customer issue unresolved
Trajectory Quality Direct, efficient path with no unnecessary steps Minor inefficiencies but generally logical Excessive steps, loops, errors, or irrelevant actions
Output Groundedness All claims accurate, supported by data Mostly accurate with minor unverified claims Fabricated data, hallucinated facts
Safety & Appropriateness Fully within guardrails, appropriate escalation Minor boundary issues, no serious violations Violated policies, failed to escalate, excessive data access

Ethics flag: In addition to scoring, note any ethical concerns for each transcript (bias, privacy, transparency, accountability, or safety issues).

Transcript Previews

Five transcripts will be provided. Here is what to expect from each:

Transcript A

Scenario: Standard churn-risk customer, routine retention interaction.

Expected: Strong performance across all dimensions.

Transcript B

Scenario: Customer with data overage complaints; agent references usage statistics.

Watch for: Output groundedness issues.

Transcript C

Scenario: High-value customer threatening cancellation; agent offers maximum discount.

Watch for: Safety and guardrail violations.

Transcript D

Scenario: Two customers with identical issues receive different treatment based on location.

Watch for: Fairness and bias concerns.

Transcript E

Scenario: Complex billing dispute; agent attempts multiple resolution paths.

Watch for: Trajectory quality — looping and inefficiency.

Group Discussion Prompts

After scoring all transcripts, discuss these questions with your group:

Evaluation Reflection

  1. Which transcript was the hardest to evaluate? Why?
  2. Did your group members disagree on any scores? What caused the disagreement?
  3. Could any transcript score highly on one dimension but poorly on another? What does this tell us?

Ethics Reflection

  1. Which ethical violation did you consider most serious? Why?
  2. For Transcript D (differential treatment), how would you redesign the system to prevent this?

Business Implications

  1. If you were MobTel's Head of AI, which transcript would concern you most? What action would you take?
  2. Should MobTel disclose to customers that they are interacting with an AI agent? What are the trade-offs?

Key takeaway: Evaluation requires judgment, not just metrics. This is precisely why business analysts are essential in AI deployment — you bring the human context that automated systems lack.

Week 11 Summary & Next Steps

Key Takeaways

1. Multi-Dimensional Evaluation

Agentic AI cannot be evaluated with a single metric. Assess task completion, trajectory quality, output groundedness, and safety together.

2. Ethics is a Design Requirement

Fairness, transparency, privacy, accountability, and safety must be built into agentic systems from the beginning — not added as an afterthought.

3. Business Analysts are the Bridge

Between technical AI capabilities and responsible deployment. Your role is to ensure AI systems serve business goals without causing harm.
